Speaking lesson · Fluency

Teacher Script — Are you a celeb worshipper?

Summarising an article & discussing questions arising · task repetition · Zoom breakout rooms
45 min Speaking · fluency 5 stages Article p.91 / p.140
Stage 1

🗓️ Lead-in — 4–5 minutes

4–5 min 👥 T–Ss (open class)
🎙️ Teacher says
"Good evening everyone, great to see you. Have a look at this."

[Show the calendar slide — this month's entertainment events.]

"This is this month's calendar — and as you can see, it's a big month for famous people: a film premiere, a music awards ceremony, a celebrity wedding, a cup final…"

DEMO — model your own answer first so students know exactly what to produce:
"For me? I always check football news — I follow my team every single day. And I admit I read about that celebrity wedding! So that's me. What about you?"

"Here's my question: which of these events would you actually follow? And be honest — is there any famous person whose news you always check? [Name], how about you?"

[Open class — nominate 2–3 Ss to answer the whole group, one after another. No breakout rooms; keep it quick and lively. React genuinely: "No way!" / "I'd never have guessed!" / "Anyone else a secret superfan?"]

"Interesting! Well, today we're going to read about people who take following celebrities much, much further…"
💡 Keep it tight — under 5 minutes. Model your own answer first (the demo), then nominate. The calendar is just a springboard; don't let the chat expand.
Stage 2

📝 Content Preparation — 6–7 minutes

6–7 min 👥 T–Ss / S
🎙️ Teacher says
[Show the article slide.]

"This is a real article from a British newspaper: 'Are you a celeb worshipper?' It's about something psychologists call Celebrity Worship Syndrome."

"Here's the task. Read the article and get ready to TALK about it. Make three or four quick notes: the main point, the key facts — who, what, how many — and one detail that surprises you. And one more thing: prepare ONE question or opinion this article raises for you. Something it makes you wonder."

"Notes only — no full sentences. When you speak, the article will be hidden, so your notes are all you'll have."

[ICQs:]
"Notes or full sentences?" [Notes]
"Apart from the summary notes — what else do you prepare?" [One question or opinion it raises]

"Five minutes — go."

[Ss read & prepare individually. Monitor on mute; help only with blocking vocabulary. 1-minute warning before time.]
💡 This stage feeds Stage 4 — the notes and the "question arising" ARE the content of the speaking. If someone finishes early, push them: "What's a second question this raises for you?"
Stage 3

💬 Language Preparation — MA(F)P — 7–8 minutes

7–8 min 👥 S–S (discovery) → T–Ss
🎙️ Meaning — guided discovery in PAIRS (not teacher-fronted)
[Show the "What's each phrase for?" slide and share the matching link in the chat.]

"Here are six phrases you can use in a moment — and six 'jobs' they do. With your partner, match each phrase to its job. Don't worry if you're not sure — have a go. Two minutes, in pairs."

[ICQ:] "Working alone or with your partner?" [Partner]

[Ss match in pairs (breakout or main-room pairs), one pair only — no switching. 2 min. Monitor.]

OCFB — check the answers by ELICITING, not telling. Go through the six, nominating pairs for their match, then check the key meaning/appropriacy points with quick CCQs:
"'The article claims that…' — which job? … And if I CLAIM something, do I fully believe it, or do I have doubts?" [Doubts — more sceptical than 'says']
"'According to the article' — whose idea is it, mine or the article's?" [The article's — so NOT 'according to me']
"'I'd be interested to know what others think' — am I giving my opinion, or asking for theirs?" [Asking for theirs — a polite way to pass the turn]
"'What interests me is whether…' — the article's idea, or MY personal angle?" [My angle]
Answer key: 1→b (most important idea) · 2→e (the article's idea, not yours) · 3→d (report it, hint doubt) · 4→a (open a question) · 5→f (your personal angle) · 6→c (politely invite opinions). Form is not analysed — these are taught as fixed chunks.
🎙️ Pronunciation — ELICIT model + features, THEN drill
[For 2–3 key chunks, don't model first — draw it out of the students, then drill.]

"The article claims that…"
"How do you think we say this naturally — can someone try it?" [Elicit an attempt from 1–2 Ss.]
"Good — now where's the strongest stress?" [Elicit: CLAIMS] "And the end of 'claims' — what sound?" [Elicit /mz/]
"Great — all together: the article CLAIMS that… Repeat!" [Now drill: choral ×2 → 2 individuals.]

"According to the article, …"
"Try it for me — how does it sound?" [Elicit attempt.] "What happens to 'to' — is it strong or weak?" [Elicit: weak, /tə/]
"Yes — according-to-the, all linked. Repeat!" [Drill choral → individual.]

"It raises the question of whether…"
"Have a go… which word might be tricky?" [Elicit: 'whether'.] "How do we say it?" [Elicit /ˈweðə/ — watch L1 /beder/]
"Perfect — 'whether', and 'of' is weak /əv/. Repeat!" [Isolate /w/ and /ð/ if needed, then drill the chunk.]

"These six phrases stay on screen while you talk. Last check — will you read the article aloud, or summarise it with these phrases?" [Summarise]
💡 The order matters: elicit the model → elicit the features (stress/weak forms) → drill. Don't say it first — let them try, shape it, then drill. Keep the whole stage to 7–8 min.
Stage 4

🗣️ Speaking ×2–3 — 10–15 minutes

10–15 min 👥 S–S · breakout rooms · new partner each round
🎙️ Teacher says
"Time to talk. Hide the article — notes only!"

DEMO — model the whole task in ~30 seconds before opening rooms so the output is crystal clear:
"Watch me first. 'The main point the article is making is that about a third of people are obsessed with a celebrity — and a small group, about two percent, would even lie or die for their hero. What interests me is whether social media has made this worse… Now I'd ask my partner: what do YOU think?'"

Then highlight the shape: "See what I did? Summary in my own words → a question it raises → I passed it to my partner. Your turn to do the same."

"In your breakout room: take turns. Summarise the story in your own words using the phrases, then share your question — 'It raises the question of whether…' — and discuss it together. React to your partner and ask follow-ups."

[ICQs:] "Article open or hidden?" [Hidden] "After the summary, what do you discuss?" [The questions it raises]

Round 1 (~5 min) — [Open breakout rooms in pairs. Monitor between rooms with mic off; NOTE good language + errors. Do not interrupt.]

[Close rooms.] "Nice work. Now — same story, NEW partner. The challenge this time: smoother, faster, better phrases. And a new angle: which of the three types — mild, moderate, serious — do you recognise in people you actually know?"

Round 2 (~5 min) — [Re-group so everyone has a new partner. Monitor and keep noting.]

[If time:] "One more round, one more partner! This time your summary in under a minute — and then tell me: is social media making all this worse?"

Round 3 (~4 min) — [New pairs again. Monitor.]
💡 The repetition IS the lesson — the second telling should be noticeably more fluent. Frame each round as a challenge, not a repeat. Keep your own talk minimal between rounds: regroup, one line of instruction, go.
Stage 5

🔍 Content & Language Feedback (DEC) — 6 minutes

6 min 👥 T–Ss / S–S
🎙️ Teacher says
"Welcome back everyone! First, the big question: honest reactions — is celebrity worship harmless fun, or a real problem? [Name]?"

[Nominate 2 Ss, ~2 min total. React to CONTENT, not language.]

"Now — while you were talking, I noted some of the language you used. Here are three sentences. Two of them have a mistake. One is already perfect — but be careful, it might look wrong! With your partner: find and fix the mistakes, and decide which one is correct. One minute."

[Show the final DEC slide with the 3 phrases. Ss work in pairs, 1 min. Monitor.]

[OCFB — elicit corrections phrase by phrase:]
"Number one — 'According to me, it's a real problem.' Is that right? … What should it be?" [Elicit: "In my opinion, …" — 'according to' is for an external source, not your own view.]
"Number two — 'The article claim that a third of people are obsessed.'" [Elicit: "claims" — third-person -s.]
"Number three — 'It raises the question of whether celebrities are bad role models.' Mistake or not?" [The trap! It is CORRECT. Confirm 'of whether' is right and praise anyone who left it alone.]

"Last thing — I want you to notice something. Your second summary was faster and smoother than your first. That's what fluency practice does. Great work today — see you next class!"
