📄 Transcript — teacher reference / post-listening

Neil & Beth: In the 1930s, people predicted "smell-o-vision" (a TV you can smell) and a miracle pill that cures all diseases — these never came true. Tech billionaire Elon Musk recently predicted that AI will eventually mean no one will have to work. Other predictions: robots will take over most jobs, and everyone will work from home.

During Covid, one prediction came true — millions were forced to work from home. Another idea is the "four-day working week", where employees work four days for the same money as five. Quiz: of 60 companies in a 2023 four-day-week trial, how many planned to keep it — 52%, 72% or 92%?

Prof Brendan Burchell (University of Cambridge), on BBC World Service's The Real Story with Shaun Ley, says we should be sceptical and take predictions "with a pinch of salt": the "track record" of economists and social scientists at predicting big changes in working time isn't good — such predictions haven't "come to pass", even if we're heading slowly in that direction.

Another expert, Andrew, is not a "dystopian" — he doesn't think AI will "get rid of" all jobs. He predicts AI will replace some jobs (those workers will need support), but new jobs will "crop up" (appear unexpectedly). That's exactly why the future is hard to predict.

Answer: a whopping 92% of the companies planned to keep the four-day week — it was popular with bosses and workers alike.
