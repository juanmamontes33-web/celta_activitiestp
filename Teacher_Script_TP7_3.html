Past Modals of Deduction

“The Mystery of Flat 4B” — making guesses about the past 🕵️

⏱️ 45 min📈 Upper-Intermediate💻 Zoom · ~8 Ss 🎯 must / might / may / could / couldn’t have + V3

🔁 Carry-over action points — keep these visible

  • Pronunciation: ELICIT before you model (“Where’s the stress? What happens to have?”), then model clearly and mark stress on screen. This is the #1 thing to show today.
  • CCQs AFTER the discovery task — don’t assume they got it. Check meaning once they’ve sorted the cline.
  • Short instructions + ICQ the mechanics before you give time or send to breakouts.
  • Protect freer practice: hard 5-minute floor in breakouts — do not cut it short.
  • Selective nomination for the bigger group; less TTT, more eliciting.
1 · Lead-inset the scene5 min
Do

Share screen → Slide 2 (the scene: cold coffee, dead phone, locked door, missing coat).

Teacher says

“Look at this picture. This is someone’s flat. Something strange is happening here.”

Teacher says — model first

“Look — the coffee is cold, the phone is dead, the door is locked. Hmm… I think the person left in a hurry. That’s my guess. What do you think happened here?”

Do

Nominate 2–3 Ss by name. Let them guess in any language they have. Type one or two guesses on the whiteboard.

Tip: Do NOT correct their grammar here. You just want their ideas + curiosity. The gaps in their language are exactly why they need today’s structures.
Teacher says — bridge

“Great theories! Today we’re going to read a mystery and learn how to make these GUESSES about the past — what happened, and how SURE we are.”

2 · Text Workgist + intensive9–10 min
▸ Gist (3–4 min)
Do

Paste the Student Activity link in the chat. Show Slide 3.

Teacher says

“Open the link. Read the story quickly — don’t stop for every word. Just answer ONE question: what was the REAL explanation for Daniel’s disappearance?”

ICQs (mechanics, then time): “Do you read fast or slow?” → Fast. · “Do you choose one answer or three?” → One. · “How long? Two minutes.”
Do

Ss read individually (2 min). Then OCFB — nominate ONE student.

Answer: B) He went on holiday (the Instagram photo from Thailand). The cat wasn’t even his! 🐱
▸ Intensive (5–6 min)
Teacher says

“Now read again, carefully. Three neighbours give their theories. I want you to decide: who is SURE, and who is just GUESSING? Underline the words that show how sure each person is.”

ICQs: “Read fast or carefully?” → Carefully. · “Do you underline one word, or the whole phrase?” → The phrase.
Do

Individually (3 min) → breakout rooms in pairs to compare (2 min). Monitor.

Do — OCFB

Elicit the three theories and type them on the whiteboard in THREE columns: sure it happened / not sure / sure it did NOT happen. Do NOT name the rule yet.

Expected: Mrs Patterson = sure it happened (“He must have left”). · Manager = guessing (“He might have gone… he may have had…”). · Mr Díaz = sure it did NOT happen (“He couldn’t have gone far”) + a guess (“He could have had an accident”).
3 · Clarification (MFP)guided discovery12 min
Principle: They DISCOVER the rules from the worksheet — you set up, monitor silently, then elicit & check. Reveal the answer-key slides only AFTER each task.
▸ Meaning — Ss discover (4 min) · Slide 5
Teacher says

“Step 1 on your worksheet. Put these three sentences on the CERTAINTY LINE — from ‘I’m sure it did NOT happen’ to ‘I’m almost sure it happened’. Then match each one to the evidence in the text.”

ICQ: “Do I tell you the answer, or do you decide?” → We decide.
Do

Individually (2 min), then compare and justify in pairs in breakout rooms (2 min). Reveal Slide 5a and elicit where each goes.

CCQs — AFTER the task (carry-over point):
must have → “Am I sure, or guessing?” Guessing — but almost sure. · “Is there evidence?” Yes — the email, the cold coffee.
might / may / could have → “Sure or not sure?” Not sure. · “Is it possible?” Yes.
couldn’t have → “Did it happen?” No. · “Am I sure?” Yes — it’s impossible.
L1 bridge (Spanish): if someone reads “must have left” as obligation, contrast: debió (de) haber salido = deduction (our meaning) vs tuvo que salir = obligation. CCQ: “Do I KNOW, or am I guessing from clues?”
▸ Form — Ss discover (4 min) · Slide 6
Teacher says

“Step 2. Look at the three sentences. What is the SAME in all of them? Complete the form rule.”

Do

Individually (2 min), then compare in pairs (1 min). Elicit the pattern FROM them, then reveal Slide 6a.

Board this:  modal + have + past participle (V3)
Define ‘have’: here it’s an auxiliary (a helper verb — no meaning of “possess”), and it never changes (never has). What comes after it? The past participle — the 3rd form of the verb (left, gone, had, been).
Quick form CCQs: “Is ‘have’ the main verb here?” → No — it’s a helper. · “After ‘have’, infinitive or past participle?” → Past participle. · “After the modal — have or has?” → have.
▸ Pronunciation — elicit → model → drill (4 min) · Slides 7 / 7a
This is your headline improvement today. Elicit FIRST.
Teacher says — say it naturally, twice

“Listen to me. He must have left in a hurry. … He must have left in a hurry. Two questions: Where am I stressing? And what happens to the word ‘have’?”

Do

Take hands / nominate. Let THEM tell you. Resist explaining first.

Guide them to notice: stress lands on the modal and the main verb (MUST… LEFT). “have” becomes a tiny weak /əv/ — “must’ve”. We write have, we say /əv/ — never “must of”.
Teacher says — model + mark

“Yes! Listen: must’ve /ˈmʌstəv/, might’ve /ˈmaɪtəv/, couldn’t’ve /ˈkʊdəntəv/.” (Reveal Slide 7a; mark the stress dots on screen.)

Teacher says — backchain drill

“Repeat after me: left … must’ve left … He must’ve left.”  → choral ×3, then nominate 3–4 individuals. Repeat with “might’ve gone” and “couldn’t’ve gone”.

Listen for: full “must HAVE” /hæv/ (unnatural) → push the weak /əv/. And the “must of” spelling trap — say it, don’t spell it.
4 · Controlled Practiceaccuracy7 min
Do

Direct Ss to Task 5 (Controlled Practice) in the same activity — no new link. Show Slide 8. Model item 1 on screen before they start.

Teacher says

“Look at number 1 with me. ‘The ground is wet this morning.’ That’s the evidence. So… ‘It must have rained during the night.’ I’m almost sure. Your turn for the other five. Read the clue, choose the modal. Compare with your partner before you check.”

ICQs: “Check alone or compare first?” → Compare first. · “How long?” → 3 minutes.
Do

Individually (3 min) → breakouts to compare (2 min). OCFB: for each, ask “Why this one? What’s the evidence?”

Key: 1 must have rained · 2 might have left · 3 couldn’t have stolen · 4 must have been · 5 might have dropped · 6 couldn’t have failed.
5 · Freer Practice + DECfluency11 min
Do

Show Slide 9 (three new mini-mysteries). Model ONE.

Teacher says — model

Model communicatively — don’t monologue. Show mystery 1 and elicit from a student first: “[Student], what do you think happened to my friend?” → build it together: “Yes! She might have overslept! What else? … She couldn’t have forgotten — we spoke yesterday!” Get 2–3 guesses from the class, then: “Now you — make as many guesses as you can for ALL three mysteries.”

ICQ: “One guess or many?” → Many.
Do — protect the time

1 min thinking → breakout rooms in pairs for a hard 5 minutes. Do not cut short. Monitor silently; collect 3–4 errors + 1 good line.

Do — OCFB

Selective nomination: 1–2 Ss share their best guess. React to the content (“Ooh, creepy!”), then annotate one strong example on screen.

▸ DEC (2 min) · Slide 10
Teacher says

“Last thing — are these correct? How do we say them?” (Elicit the correction from Ss before you confirm.)

Corrections: “He must left → must have left.” · “She must of → must have forgotten.” · “He couldn’t have went → gone far.” · “They might has → might have arrived.”
Teacher says — close

“Brilliant deducing today, detectives. You can now guess about the past AND show how sure you are. See you next class!” 🕵️
