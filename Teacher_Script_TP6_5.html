Teacher Script — Two Witnesses, Two Stories

Telling a story + comparing accounts · 6 chunks · 5 stages · Online Zoom · TP5 feedback applied ✓
45 min Upper-Intermediate (B2) Functional language No controlled practice — freer only
⚠️ TP5 Feedback — Apply in every stage

Five things to nail this time

1. Be VISUAL with pronunciation 🗣️
Show stress + linking on screen. MODEL it clearly yourself before drilling. (Flagged twice!)
2. Ask more CCQs 🎯
Confirm meaning with yes/no CCQs for each chunk — don't just tell them.
3. React when monitoring 👂
Listen in BORs and bring real stories back into feedback. Show you heard them.
4. Scaffold the freer task 🪜
Strong demo + phrases on screen before they speak (no controlled stage to lean on).
5. Ask follow-ups in feedback ➕
In OCFB, push for elaboration: "Why? What happened next? Who was right?"
Stage 1

🎯 Lead-in — 4–5 minutes

4–5 min👥 T–Ss (open class)
🎙️ Teacher says
"Hi everyone! Quick question to start. Look at this — same street, same moment, two people watching."

[Show the title/lead-in slide with the two conflicting witness bubbles.]

DEMO first — model a 20-second mini-story so they know what to share:
"Last week I saw a tiny accident — a man dropped his coffee all over a cyclist. I told my wife one thing… but the waiter told her something completely different! Same event, two stories."

"So — have you ever seen something happen, and then heard people describe it in totally different ways? What happened? [Name], how about you?"

[Nominate 2–3 Ss, open class. React genuinely: "No way!" / "So who was right?" Keep it brisk.]

"Great — today, two friends are trying to work out what REALLY happened outside a café. Let's read."
💡 Open class, not breakout — it's a 4-minute warmer. Model first, then nominate.
Stage 2

📖 Context / Text — Gist read — 6–7 minutes

6–7 min👥 T–Ss / S / T–Ss
🎙️ Teacher says
"Open the link in the chat — Section 1. Read the conversation between Mara and Dan. Don't worry about every word — just the main idea. Two questions: What happened outside the café? And why can't they agree?"

[Show the READING INSTRUCTIONS slide (the two questions). Send the discovery link. ICQs — mechanics first:]
"Are you reading for every detail, or the main idea?" [Main idea]
"How many questions?" [Two]

[Ss read individually, ~3 min. Then quick pair-check (chat or open class).]

[Reveal the READING ANSWER-KEY slide. OCFB — nominate, and ASK FOLLOW-UPS:]
"[Name] — what happened?" [A cyclist crashed when a car door opened.] "And why can't Mara and Dan agree? Who did they hear it from?" [Two witnesses — the waiter and the old lady — told different versions.]
💡 Keep gist short — one read, two questions, quick OCFB. The text just sets up the language; don't over-analyse content here.
Stage 3

🔎 Language Clarification — GD (MFP) — 13–14 minutes

13–14 min👥 S–S (discovery) → T–Ss
How to read this: green italic = say it out loud, word for word.  [grey] = what to do / the answer you're listening for.  The flow is always the same: students discover in the activity → you reveal the answer slide → you ask the questions below and they answer.
🎙️ A · SORT THE PHRASES (Section 2 of the link)
[Show the "Sort the phrases" INSTRUCTIONS slide — NOT the answer key yet. Make sure the link is in the chat.]

"Okay, look at the screen. You can see six phrases — these all come from the conversation you just read. Some of them we use to TELL a story, and some we use to COMPARE two different versions of a story. With your partner, drag each phrase into the correct group. You have two minutes."

[ICQ before they start:] "Are you working alone, or with your partner?" [With my partner]   "How many minutes?" [Two]

"Great — off you go!"
[Ss sort in pairs, 2 min. Monitor silently — just see how they're doing, don't correct yet.]

[Bring them back. NOW change to the SORT ANSWER-KEY slide.]
"Okay, let's check together. Look at the screen — these three are for TELLING the story: 'was just …-ing when', 'all of a sudden', and 'in the end'. And these three are for COMPARING: 'according to', 'claimed that', and 'was absolutely certain that'. Did you get them right? Great."
🎙️ B · MEANING (Section 3 of the link) — they match, then you check with CCQs
[Show the "Match the meaning" INSTRUCTIONS slide. Keep the link open at Section 3.]

"Now Section three. This time, each line is a sentence from the conversation, and the phrase is in blue. On the right, drag the meaning that matches each sentence. Talk to your partner — why do you think it matches? You have three minutes."

[ICQ:] "Do you write a sentence, or drag a meaning?" [Drag a meaning]

"Perfect — go ahead!"
[Ss match in pairs, ~3 min. Monitor.]

[Bring them back. Change to the MEANING ANSWER-KEY slide. Now go phrase by phrase. Say the phrase, ask the question, WAIT for them to answer, then confirm. Don't answer your own questions.]

1 · "He was just riding when…"
"Look at number one. 'The cyclist was just riding when the door opened.' Let me ask you — when the door opened, was he already riding, or did he start riding at that moment?" [WAIT → "Already riding."]
"Exactly. He was already riding. And then — did something interrupt him?" [WAIT → "Yes, the door."]
"Right! So we use this to show a longer action that was happening… and then — bang — something interrupts it. Good."

2 · "All of a sudden…"
"Number two — 'all of a sudden the cyclist flew over the handlebars.' Did this happen slowly, or very quickly?" [WAIT → "Quickly."]
"And did anyone expect it?" [WAIT → "No."]
"Perfect. 'All of a sudden' means something quick and surprising. Nobody expected it."

3 · "In the end…"
"Number three — 'in the end, the cyclist stood up and walked away.' Is this the BEGINNING of the story, or the final result?" [WAIT → "The final result."]
"Yes — it comes AFTER everything else. It's how the story finishes. Good."

4 · "According to…"
"Now the comparing phrases. Number four — 'according to the waiter, the cyclist was riding past.' Is this MY opinion, or is it someone else's?" [WAIT → "Someone else's — the waiter's."]
"Exactly. It's the waiter's version, not mine. So — can I say 'according to me, I'm hungry'?" [WAIT → "No!"]
"Right, we don't say 'according to me'. For my own opinion I say 'in MY opinion'. 'According to' is always for someone ELSE."

5 · "…claimed that…"
"Number five — 'the waiter claimed that the driver wasn't looking.' Do we KNOW that's one hundred percent true?" [WAIT → "No / we're not sure."]
"Good. He SAYS it… but maybe it's true, maybe it's not. When I say 'claimed', I'm telling you I'm not totally sure it's true."

6 · "…was absolutely certain that…"
"Last one — 'she was absolutely certain that the cyclist was too fast.' Was she a little bit sure, or completely sure?" [WAIT → "Completely sure."]
"Yes — one hundred percent, no doubt at all. 'Absolutely certain' is the strongest — she has zero doubt."

"Great work — you understand what they all mean. Now let's see how they're built."
💡 The golden rule: ask the question → stop talking → let them answer → THEN confirm. If you answer your own question, it stops being a CCQ. Two questions per phrase is plenty.
🎙️ C · FORM (Section 4 of the link) — they label, then you check with FCQs
[Show the "Notice the pattern" INSTRUCTIONS slide. The link is at Section 4 — the dark drag-the-parts task.]

"Last task before you speak. Look at Section four. The yellow part — the START of each phrase — is already there for you, because it never changes. Your job: drag the missing pieces into the empty boxes — the person or thing, the action, and the rest. Work with your partner. Three minutes."

[ICQ:] "Does the yellow part change?" [No — it's fixed]   "What do you drag in?" [The person, the action, the rest]

"Perfect — go!"
[Ss label in pairs, ~3 min. Monitor — are they noticing the start is always fixed?]

[Bring them back. Change to the FORM ANSWER-KEY slide. Now elicit the pattern. Use simple words — say "person or thing" and "action", NOT "subject", "verb" or "clause".]

"Okay, let's look at the pattern together. Take 'He CLAIMED THAT…' — point at it. Does this start, 'he claimed that', change? Or is it always the same?" [WAIT → "Always the same / fixed."]
"Exactly — it's FIXED. We keep it exactly like that every time. Now, right after 'claimed that', what comes first — a person or thing, or an action?" [WAIT → "A person/thing — 'the driver'."]
"Good. And after the person?" [WAIT → "The action — 'wasn't looking'."]
"Perfect. So the pattern is always: the FIXED start… then a PERSON or thing… then an ACTION. Let's check it works for the others."

[Point to each on the answer-key slide and confirm quickly — same pattern every time:]
"'According to' — fixed — then a person, 'the waiter' — then the action. 'All of a sudden' — fixed — 'the cyclist' — 'flew'. 'In the end' — fixed — 'the cyclist' — 'walked'. 'She was absolutely certain that' — fixed — 'the cyclist' — 'was going'. See? Every single one: fixed start, then person, then action."

"So the good news — you don't build these word by word. You learn the START as one fixed chunk, and then you just add your own person and your own action. That's it."
💡 Keep the metalanguage simple: "fixed start → person/thing → action → the rest". Never say "subject", "verb", or "clause" — they'll get lost. The whole point is that the start is a FIXED chunk they can reuse.
🎙️ D · PRONUNCIATION (your slide) — you model, they repeat
[Show YOUR pronunciation slide (stress highlighted + linking arcs). For each phrase: SAY IT first yourself, clearly and naturally — then "Repeat!" — then they say it together (choral) — then pick one or two students. Point at the stress on the slide as you say it.]

1 · "he wəz just RIDing when"  /wəz dʒʌst/
"Listen to me first — 'he was just RIDing when'. Notice 'was' is very small and quick — /wəz/, not 'WOZ'. And the strong word is RIDing. … Now you: repeat!" [Choral. Then: "Just you, [name]?" → one or two individuals.]

2 · "all‿of‿a‿SUDDen"  /ˌɔːl əv ə ˈsʌdn/
"Listen — 'all of a SUDDen'. Hear how 'of a' almost disappears? all-of-a-SUDDen — it's all joined together, and the strong part is SUDD. … Repeat!" [Choral → individual. If it's messy, build it backwards: "SUDDen" → "a-SUDDen" → "of-a-SUDDen" → "all-of-a-SUDDen".]

3 · "in‿the‿END"  /ɪn ðiː ˈend/
"'In the END'. The strong word is END, and it's all linked — in-the-END. … Repeat!" [Choral → individual.]

4 · "aCORding‿to"  /əˈkɔːdɪŋ tə/
"'aCORding to'. The strong part is COR — a-COR-ding. And 'to' is tiny and quick — /tə/, joined on the end. Not 'TWO'. … Repeat!" [Choral → individual. Watch they don't say a strong 'TOO'.]

5 · "CLAIMED that"  /kleɪmd ðət/
"'CLAIMED that'. Careful — it's ONE sound at the end: 'claimed', not 'claim-ED', not two syllables. CLAIMED. … Repeat!" [Choral → individual. Listen for the /-md/ ending.]

6 · "absoLUTEly CERtain"  /ˌæbsəˈluːtli ˈsɜːtn/
"'absoLUTEly CERtain'. Two strong parts — absoLUTEly… CERtain. … Repeat!" [If long, do it in two halves: "absoLUTEly" → "CERtain" → join them. Then individual.]

"Excellent — that sounds really natural now. Okay, time to use all of this and tell some stories!"
💡 The order never changes: you say it → "Repeat!" → everyone together → one or two on their own. Always model with YOUR voice first — they copy you, not the slide. Point at the stress as you say it.
Stage 4

💬 Freer Practice (× 2) — 13–15 minutes

13–15 min👥 T–Ss / S–S (breakout, new partner R2)
🎙️ Teacher says
SCAFFOLD + DEMO — model the whole task (~30s) before opening rooms:
"Watch me first. Last year I was just walking home when, all of a sudden, a dog ran past with a sausage in its mouth! In the end the owner caught it. — And the comparing part: According to my neighbour, the dog was hers; but the butcher claimed that it was a street dog!"

[Point to each phrase on the slide as you use it. Then:]

PLANNING — give them thinking time (1–2 min) before they speak: "Before we go to rooms — take one minute. Think of YOUR story and write down just 4–5 key words you'll need. Not full sentences — just words. What happened, where, the surprise, the ending."
[ICQ:] "Full sentences, or just words?" [Just words] [Give 1–2 min of quiet thinking/writing time. This is the scaffold — weaker Ss especially need it to speak fluently.]

Round 1 — Tell your story. "In your breakout room: tell your partner about a time something dramatic or unexpected happened to YOU. Use the telling phrases on screen, and your key words. Partner — listen, and ask one or two questions. Five minutes!"
[ICQ:] "Your story, or your partner's?" [Mine]
[Open BORs. MONITOR and REACT — note good language + errors AND remember whose story is whose for feedback.]

Round 2 — New partner, compare. [Re-group so everyone has a NEW partner.] "Now tell your new partner about the stories you just heard — use the comparing phrases: 'According to Ana… She claimed that… but Luis was absolutely certain that…' Five minutes!"
[This creates an information gap — the new partner didn't hear the original stories. Monitor, keep noting language.]
💡 No controlled practice before this — so the demo, the 1–2 min planning time, and the phrases-on-screen ARE the scaffold. Don't skip the planning step; it's what Beto asked for. React to what you hear — you'll use it in feedback.
Stage 5

✅ Content & Language Feedback (DEC) — 6 minutes

6 min👥 T–Ss / S–S
🎙️ Teacher says
As Ss talk in feedback, ANNOTATE their contributions on a shared slide/whiteboard — jot key content + good phrases they used + a couple of errors. You'll feed these into the DEC.
"Welcome back! Some great stories in there." [React to specific ones you heard while monitoring:] "Ana — I loved yours. Tell everyone — what were the two versions? … And who do you think was really right?"
[Nominate 1–2 Ss, ask FOLLOW-UPS to push elaboration, annotating as they speak. ~2 min.]

"Now — look at the board. Here are some sentences I noticed while you were talking. Two have a mistake; one is already perfect — careful, it might look wrong! With your partner: find and fix the mistakes, and decide which one is correct. One minute."

[Show the Find & Fix slide. Ss work in pairs, 1 min. Then OCFB — elicit:]
"Number one — 'According to me, the dog was fast.'" [Elicit: "In my opinion…" — 'according to' is for a source, not yourself.]
"Number two — 'He was walk home when the car stopped.'" [Elicit: "was walking" — needs -ing.]
"Number three — 'She was absolutely certain that he was lying.'" [The trap — it's CORRECT! Praise anyone who left it.]

"Excellent work today. Your stories were great and the comparing phrases are sounding really natural. See you next time!"
DEC answer key 🔑
1. "According to me…" ✘ → "In my opinion…" (according to = a source, not yourself; L1 según yo)
2. "He was walk…" ✘ → "He was walking…" (was + …-ing)
3. "She was absolutely certain that…" ✔ CORRECT — the trap, nothing to change
💡 TP5 fixes here: REACT to real stories from monitoring + ask follow-up questions to help Ss elaborate.
